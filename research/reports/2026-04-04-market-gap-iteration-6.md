## Honest Assessment: All Three Verticals Have Limited Micro-SaaS Opportunity

### Fitness/Health: Well-Served at Every Tier

The fitness software space in 2026 is mature and competitive. The personal trainer platform market alone has 15+ tools covering workout programming, nutrition, check-ins, scheduling, and payments. Prices range from free (TrainerFu) to $225/mo (Trainerize Studio Plus). The "Trainerize is too complex" complaint is real, but TrueCoach, Everfit, and FitBudd already serve the "simpler" segment.

The most interesting signal was **credit-based scheduling for sports trainers**, where CoachIQ ($50-150/mo) appears to be the only purpose-built solution. Generic tools (Acuity, Calendly, MindBody) don't handle credit packs properly. However, CoachIQ is already solving this and has strong content marketing/SEO presence.

The **fitness challenge delivery** space (messaging-based challenges via WhatsApp/Telegram) has been claimed by CommuniPass ($29/mo, free tier) which nails the exact pain point: 25% of fitness apps are abandoned after one use, but WhatsApp-delivered challenges get 70-80% completion rates. No room for a second player.

### Event Management: Thoroughly Saturated

The event management space is a $15.2B market in 2026 with 30+ platforms. The "Eventbrite is too expensive" gap has been filled by at least 12 alternatives with lower fees. TixFox at $0.39/ticket flat is 7.4x cheaper than Eventbrite. Hi.Events is 78% cheaper. Ticket Tailor, SimpleTix, Yapsody, Zeffy all serve different segments. For virtual events, free tiers from Airmeet and Contrast cover small organizers. This space has been "Intercom-ed" -- the gap was real 3 years ago and is now thoroughly filled.

### Supply Chain/Operations: Wrong Scale for Micro-SaaS

Supply chain visibility is enterprise territory (FourKites, Project44). Small business inventory management has 14+ free tools. The spreadsheet-to-MRP gap for small manufacturers is real (MRPeasy at $49/mo is the cheapest) but requires 3-6 months to build BOM/production features -- not feasible as a 1-2 week MVP. Food truck/restaurant operations are dominated by free POS software (Square). None of these are micro-SaaS opportunities.

## Three Ideas Analyzed in Depth

Despite the saturation, I analyzed the three least-bad opportunities.

### Idea 1: SessionPack -- Credit-Based Booking for Independent Sports Coaches

**Problem**: Independent sports coaches (basketball trainers, hitting coaches, pitching instructors, tennis pros) run credit-based businesses: athletes buy session packs (e.g., 10 sessions for $500), then book individual sessions against their credit balance. Generic scheduling tools (Acuity, Calendly, Square Appointments) don't support credit packs -- they handle one-off bookings or subscriptions, but not "deduct 1 credit per 1-on-1, 0.5 credits per group session." Coaches manage credits manually in spreadsheets alongside scheduling in Acuity, creating a fragmented workflow. No-show rates run 15-22% because athletes don't feel the cost of missing a pre-paid session.

**Who has it**: Independent sports coaches and small training facilities (1-3 coaches) serving youth/amateur athletes. Think: the local basketball trainer running sessions at a rented gym, the batting cage owner offering private lessons. Estimated 200K+ independent sports coaches in the US alone.

**Why current solutions fail**:
- **Acuity/Calendly/Square Appointments**: No credit pack concept. Can't set different credit costs per session type. No low-balance alerts. Designed for service businesses, not sports training.
- **Mindbody** ($139-699/mo): Overkill for a solo coach. Designed for studios/gyms with classes, memberships, retail. Complex setup.
- **Trainerize/TrueCoach** ($20-225/mo): Designed for workout programming and online coaching, not in-person session scheduling with credit packs. The scheduling is an afterthought.
- **CoachIQ** ($50-150/mo): **This is the direct competitor.** Purpose-built for private sports coaches with credit-based booking, athlete portal, and website builder. Strong content marketing. Has case studies (Hoop House basketball). This is the biggest risk factor -- CoachIQ already exists and is well-positioned.
- **Gap (very thin)**: CoachIQ at $50-150/mo may be overpriced for solo coaches just starting out who only need scheduling + credit packs (no website builder, no CRM, no automations). A stripped-down $19-29/mo tool focused ONLY on credit-based scheduling + athlete self-booking could undercut CoachIQ. But this is the "cheaper alternative to a niche tool" play -- historically thin.

**MVP scope (2 weeks)**:
1. Coach creates session types (1-on-1, small group, etc.) with credit costs
2. Coach creates credit packs ($200/5 sessions, $350/10 sessions, etc.)
3. Athletes buy credit packs via Stripe checkout
4. Athletes self-book sessions from coach's availability, credits auto-deduct
5. Low-balance alerts to athletes and coach
6. Simple dashboard: upcoming sessions, credit balances, revenue
7. Stripe billing for coach subscription

**Revenue model**: Free tier (1 coach, 10 athletes, basic scheduling). Pro $29/mo (unlimited athletes, credit packs, low-balance alerts, no-show tracking). Facility $59/mo (3 coaches, multi-location, analytics).

**Realistic MRR ceiling**: $2K-$5K MRR. The TAM is real (200K+ independent sports coaches) but CoachIQ already owns the SEO and content marketing for this niche. Their blog ranks for every relevant keyword. Competing as "cheaper CoachIQ" is a race to the bottom. The niche is also narrow -- sports coaches who use credit packs are a subset of all trainers.

**Why Jobelo's skills give an edge**: Payment/billing systems (Stripe integration), scheduling logic, Next.js dashboard. But this is a commodity build -- no AI, no data pipeline, no technical moat.

**Kill criteria**: If CoachIQ drops pricing below $50/mo. If fewer than 30 coaches sign up in 60 days. If coaches say "I just use Acuity + a spreadsheet and it's fine."

### Idea 2: GigTix -- Ultra-Simple Event Ticketing for Micro-Events (< 100 Attendees)

**Problem**: Organizers of micro-events (comedy open mics, poetry slams, neighborhood block parties, workshop hosts, local meetup organizers) running events with < 100 attendees find that even the cheapest ticketing platforms are designed for larger events. Eventbrite's fees eat into thin margins ($3.64 per $50 ticket). More importantly, the setup friction is high -- Eventbrite requires creating an "organization," choosing from 15+ event categories, setting up a full event page with banner images. For a weekly comedy night or a monthly craft workshop, the overhead per event is disproportionate. Many organizers fall back to Venmo/Cash App + a Google Form, losing attendee data, analytics, and professional appearance.

**Who has it**: Micro-event organizers running recurring small events: comedy hosts, workshop facilitators, local meetup organizers, church event coordinators, neighborhood associations. Estimated hundreds of thousands in the US alone.

**Why current solutions fail**:
- **Eventbrite** (3.7% + $1.79/ticket): Expensive for cheap tickets ($10-25 range). Setup is heavyweight for a 30-person comedy night. The marketplace discovery benefit is minimal for recurring community events that rely on existing audiences.
- **TixFox** ($0.39/ticket flat): Cheapest fees. Good feature set including reserved seating. But still designed for "events" -- setup assumes a one-off event with a full page, description, banner image. Not optimized for "I run this every Tuesday."
- **Ticket Tailor** (~5%): Flat-fee model. Good for recurring events but still full event-page setup.
- **Luma** (free): Closest to the micro-event use case. Simple, clean, free for basic events. But monetization for Luma is through premium features for larger events. Free tier is generous.
- **Hi.Events** (0.75% + $0.40): Open-source, self-hostable. Good for tech-savvy organizers but requires more setup.
- **Zeffy** (free for nonprofits): Only for registered nonprofits.
- **Gap (extremely thin)**: Luma (free) already serves micro-events well. TixFox at $0.39/ticket is cheap enough that fee savings aren't a compelling differentiator. The "recurring event template" angle (set it up once, clone weekly) exists in Ticket Tailor. The market has been thoroughly "Intercom-ed."

**MVP scope (1 week)**:
1. Create event in 60 seconds: name, date, time, location, ticket price, quantity
2. Shareable link (no full event page required)
3. Stripe checkout for attendees
4. Recurring event: auto-clone weekly/monthly
5. Attendee list + check-in via QR code
6. Simple analytics: tickets sold, revenue, repeat attendees

**Revenue model**: Free (unlimited free events). Paid $9/mo (paid ticketing, no per-ticket fees, recurring events, analytics). Pro $19/mo (custom branding, multiple organizers, export data).

**Realistic MRR ceiling**: $1K-$3K MRR. The price point ($9-19/mo) is low. The audience (micro-event organizers) is fragmented and hard to reach at scale. Luma (free) and TixFox ($0.39/ticket) set the floor. The "simplicity" differentiator is marginal -- TixFox and Luma are already simple. There's no clear reason a micro-event organizer would pay $9/mo when TixFox charges $0.39/ticket (which on a 30-person weekly event is only $12/month in fees -- barely more than a $9/mo subscription).

**Why Jobelo's skills give an edge**: Stripe/payment integration, Next.js, QR code generation. But this is a commodity build with zero technical moat.

**Kill criteria**: If Luma remains free and adequate. If fewer than 50 organizers sign up in 60 days. If unit economics don't work (low MRR ceiling + high churn from seasonal organizers).

### Idea 3: StockSnap -- Spreadsheet-to-Inventory App for Product Businesses (< 500 SKUs)

**Problem**: Small product businesses (Etsy sellers, Shopify stores with physical inventory, small wholesalers, craft businesses, market vendors) manage inventory in spreadsheets. At 50-200 SKUs across 1-3 locations (home office, storage unit, market booth), spreadsheets break: no barcode scanning, no real-time stock updates when selling across channels, no low-stock alerts, no audit trail, and manual entry errors cause overselling. NetSuite reports that manual inventory management adds 2+ hours of daily admin. But existing inventory tools (Sortly $24/mo, Zoho Inventory free tier, Square free tier) either bundle too many features (Zoho = order management + shipping + accounting) or require buying into an ecosystem (Square = POS + payments + banking).

**Who has it**: Small product businesses with 50-500 SKUs that have outgrown spreadsheets but don't need full order management, shipping, or accounting integration. They want: scan barcode, adjust stock, get low-stock alerts, see stock across locations. That's it.

**Why current solutions fail**:
- **Sortly** ($24/mo Advanced): Closest to the "simple inventory" concept. Visual/folder-based organization. Mobile barcode scanning. But designed for asset tracking (office equipment, tools) more than product inventory. No sales channel integration. No COGS tracking.
- **Zoho Inventory** (free for 50 orders/mo): Good free tier but bundles order management, shipping integration, and accounting. The UI assumes e-commerce workflow (purchase orders, sales orders, shipments). Overkill for someone who just needs stock counting.
- **Square** (free): Inventory is tied to Square POS/payment ecosystem. Can't use just the inventory feature standalone.
- **inFlow** ($110/mo): Too expensive for small product businesses. Designed for wholesalers/distributors with purchase orders and customer management.
- **Cin7** ($349/mo): Enterprise. Way too expensive and complex.
- **Google Sheets + barcode scanner app**: The actual incumbent. Free. Works. But breaks at scale, no alerts, no multi-user, no audit trail.
- **Gap (moderate)**: There's a genuine gap between "Google Sheets" and "Sortly/Zoho" for businesses that want ONLY inventory management -- barcode scanning, stock levels, low-stock alerts, multi-location -- without order management, shipping, accounting, or POS bundling. But 20+ free inventory tools exist (SaaSWorthy lists 20), and the "simple inventory app" concept has been attempted many times. The willingness to pay for "just inventory" when Zoho offers it free (with extras) is questionable.

**MVP scope (2 weeks)**:
1. Add products: name, SKU, barcode, category, cost, price, stock quantity, location
2. Mobile barcode scanning to add/adjust stock
3. Multi-location support (warehouse A, warehouse B, market booth)
4. Low-stock alerts (email + push notification)
5. Stock history log (audit trail: who changed what, when)
6. Dashboard: total inventory value, low-stock items, stock movement trends
7. CSV import from existing spreadsheets
8. Stripe billing

**Revenue model**: Free tier (50 SKUs, 1 location, basic scanning). Pro $19/mo (500 SKUs, 3 locations, low-stock alerts, audit trail). Business $39/mo (unlimited SKUs, 10 locations, team access, CSV export).

**Realistic MRR ceiling**: $3K-$8K MRR. The TAM is massive (millions of small product businesses worldwide). The pain is real (spreadsheets break at 100+ SKUs). But the willingness to pay $19/mo when Zoho Inventory is free and Sortly is $24/mo with more features is the core risk. The "just inventory, nothing else" positioning is clean but may be too narrow -- once customers need order management or channel sync, they'll churn to Zoho/Cin7.

**Why Jobelo's skills give an edge**: Mobile app development (if Flutter -- he has costs-tracker in Flutter), barcode/camera integration, database design, API/dashboard. The barcode scanning + real-time stock sync is a mildly technical build.

**Kill criteria**: If fewer than 100 signups in 60 days. If customers say "Zoho Inventory free tier does this." If churn exceeds 15% monthly as customers outgrow "just inventory" and need order management.
